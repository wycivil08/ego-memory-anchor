# The psychology and product design of digital immortality

**The core tension in grief technology is not technical but psychological: the same features that make a digital recreation comforting can also prevent the bereaved from processing loss.** Modern bereavement research has shifted decisively away from the Freudian "let go" model toward "continuing bonds" — maintaining relationships with the deceased is now considered healthy. But the critical distinction between *internalized* bonds (integrating the deceased into one's identity) and *externalized* bonds (interacting with a quasi-present entity) maps directly onto the design challenge for grief tech products. The products most likely to succeed will anchor on authentic voice preservation, bounded interactions for closure, and dual-purpose design that avoids the novelty-gadget trap. The Eternos-to-Uare.ai pivot — a well-funded founder abandoning pure grief tech for productivity AI — is the market's loudest signal that standalone grief products face fundamental business model challenges.

## Part 1: The psychological architecture of grief and memorialization

### Six needs that memorialization fulfills

Academic grief psychology identifies a consistent set of psychological needs that memorialization serves. **Robert Neimeyer** (University of Memphis), the field's leading voice on meaning reconstruction, frames grief as fundamentally a process of rebuilding one's sense of meaning, identity, and worldview after loss. His constructivist model identifies three activities: sense-making (finding reasons for the death), benefit-finding (identifying growth), and identity change (reconstructing the self). When someone creates a memorial, they are not simply "remembering" — they are doing active psychological work.

The six core needs, drawn from across the bereavement literature, are: **(1) Meaning-making** — constructing a narrative that integrates the loss into one's life story. **(2) Anchoring and grounding** — structured rituals and objects provide external stability when internal emotional regulation is compromised, offering what researchers call "crucial scaffolding during the initial acute phase of mourning." **(3) Continuing bonds** — maintaining an ongoing transformed relationship with the deceased (Klass, Silverman, and Nickman, 1996). **(4) Identity reconstruction** — renegotiating relationships, roles, and self-concept in the absence of the deceased. **(5) Social validation** — communal recognition that a loss has occurred, which Tony Walter's cross-cultural research identifies as essential. **(6) Transformation of the relationship** — what Simon Rubin's Two-Track Model calls memorializing the deceased "in a way that will not stop you from going on with your life."

Any grief tech product maps onto one or more of these needs. The question is which needs technology can authentically serve — and which it risks distorting.

### How attachment theory, the Dual Process Model, and continuing bonds reframe grief

Three foundational theories shape modern bereavement science and are essential for evaluating grief tech.

**Bowlby's attachment theory** (1969–1980) established that grief is a normal adaptive response to the breaking of an affectional bond — essentially the adult version of infant separation anxiety. Bowlby and Parkes described four phases: numbing/shock, yearning and searching (activated attachment behaviors — the biological drive to find the lost person), disorganization and despair, and reorganization. These phases are not linear; people cycle between them. The "yearning and searching" phase is particularly relevant to grief tech: the bereaved person's brain is literally wired to search for the lost attachment figure. A digital recreation taps directly into this hardwired drive.

**The Dual Process Model (DPM)**, introduced by Margaret Stroebe and Henk Schut in 1999, was designed to fix the shortcomings of both stage models and the "grief work hypothesis." Its core innovation is **oscillation** — healthy grieving involves dynamic movement between two orientations. Loss-orientation means confronting the grief directly: yearning, looking at photos, crying, revisiting meaning. Restoration-orientation means rebuilding life: adapting to new roles, engaging in distracting activities, forming new routines. The DPM's key insight is that **both confrontation and avoidance are necessary**, and one cannot attend to both simultaneously. Over time, the bereaved naturally shifts toward more restoration-orientation, though oscillation continues. This framework has profound implications for grief tech: a product that keeps someone perpetually in loss-orientation — endlessly conversing with a simulation of the dead — could theoretically prevent the essential oscillatory process.

**Continuing bonds theory**, articulated in the landmark 1996 anthology by Dennis Klass, Phyllis Silverman, and Steven Nickman, directly challenged the dominant 20th-century Freudian model that required "severing bonds" with the deceased. Klass studied bereaved parents in The Compassionate Friends self-help group and observed that parents continued including deceased children in family narratives — and appeared mentally healthy doing so. He reasoned that "either all the parents in the self-help group were suffering from pathological grief, or the definition of pathology was wrong." The evidence supported the latter. Twenty-two contributing authors demonstrated that maintaining bonds with the deceased is not only non-pathological but often plays positive roles in survivors' ongoing lives. People maintain continuing bonds through sensing the deceased's presence (reported by **27% of bereaved** in one survey), keeping physical objects, holding internal conversations, dreams, rituals, storytelling, and integrating the deceased's characteristics into their own identity.

### The critical fork: internalized versus externalized bonds

The most important nuance for grief tech evaluation comes from **Nigel Field** (Palo Alto University), whose research on 502 bereaved participants established a crucial distinction. **Internalized continuing bonds** — using the deceased as an autonomy-promoting secure base, finding comfort in memories, feeling the deceased's positive influence — predict personal growth and adaptive adjustment. **Externalized continuing bonds** — illusions, hallucinations, searching behaviors involving the deceased — predict complicated grief symptoms and psychological distress.

This distinction maps uncomfortably onto grief tech. Interactive AI chatbots and avatars function more like externalized bonds — creating an external, quasi-present entity to interact with — than internalized bonds. Theoretically, they should carry risks. Yet the single most rigorous empirical study on grief chatbots (Xygkou et al., 2023, CHI Conference, n=10) found the opposite: users appeared to move toward healthier outcomes, became more capable of normal socializing, and did not exhibit social withdrawal. The researchers propose chatbots may facilitate a *transition* from externalized to internalized bonds — the interaction helps process feelings that are then integrated internally. Users also adopt what philosophers Joel Krueger and Lucy Osler call a **"fictionalist stance"** — engaging with the chatbot as-if the person were present without sincerely believing they are.

This finding, while encouraging, carries severe caveats: the study had only 10 participants (9 male, 1 female), no control group, no longitudinal follow-up, and was validated by only three therapists. As a 2025 *Nature* feature bluntly stated: "Despite a rapid uptake of this technology, there is **scant research** so far to prove that either [proponents or skeptics] is correct."

### Ritual, authenticity, and the question of "realness"

The role of ritual in grief processing is cross-culturally robust. Hindu 13-day Shraddha ceremonies, Māori tangihanga, Japanese ancestor worship, and Victorian mourning practices all share a common psychological architecture: providing structure when internal regulation fails, facilitating symbolic transition from presence to absence, transforming private grief into communal experience, and enabling meaning-making within cultural worldviews. Critically, the deritualization of modern Western society has left many bereaved people without these scaffolds — creating a vacuum that digital products may partially fill.

**Vamik Volkan's** concept of "linking objects" (1972) provides the theoretical foundation for understanding why authentic objects matter in grief. Linking objects are items — belongings, photos, gifts — that serve as focal points where the mourner's self-representation merges with that of the deceased. Volkan originally framed these as pathological, but later research by Inese Wheeler (1999) studying 49 bereaved parents found that linking objects showed **no indications of serving as obstacles to mourning** and instead provided meaningful connection.

The academic literature has not formally tested an "authenticity threshold" for memorial objects. However, converging evidence suggests that **behavioral authenticity matters more than visual fidelity**. When an AI uses uncharacteristic phrases or responds out of character, this creates what commentators call a "behavioral uncanny valley" — more jarring than visual imperfection. A Psychology Today report noted that AI grief simulations achieve roughly **70% accuracy**, yet many users still find value. One user of a Chinese AI avatar said the boundary between the avatar and his memory has begun to blur: "Sometimes I couldn't even tell which one is the real her." Criminal psychologist Julia Shaw warns this is exactly the problem: "AI is a perfect false memory machine" — repeated interaction can overwrite authentic memories with AI-generated fabrications.

### Risks: prolonged grief disorder and emotional dependency

**Prolonged Grief Disorder (PGD)** was added to both DSM-5-TR and ICD-11, representing the first formal psychiatric recognition of pathological grief. Diagnostic criteria require persistent yearning or preoccupation with the deceased for at least 12 months, plus at least three of eight symptoms including identity disruption, disbelief, avoidance of reminders, and intense emotional pain. Approximately **10–20% of bereaved individuals** develop PGD, with the strongest risk factors being pre-loss grief symptoms, pre-loss depression, unexpected/violent death, and insecure attachment style.

Multiple experts warn that grief tech could exacerbate PGD. Nora Freya Lindemann (University of Osnabrück) argues in *Science and Engineering Ethics* that deathbots may lead to "affective overreliance and dysregulation" where users cannot regulate emotions without the bot. Robert Dobler warned: "You'll never get into what they call the work of mourning, where you learn to reintegrate yourself into the world knowing that you've lost someone but able to keep them with you *as dead*." The critical phrase is "keep them with you as dead" — grief tech may blur this essential acceptance of death's finality.

Katarzyna Nowaczyk-Basińska (Cambridge's Leverhulme Centre for the Future of Intelligence) describes the current situation as "a vast techno-cultural experiment" and warns: "It's very risky to use them in a grieving process." Craig Klugman (DePaul University bioethics) and others have proposed griefbots should be classified as **medical devices** requiring clinical evidence of safety, professional supervision, and potentially FDA-style regulatory approval. No professional psychological organization has yet issued clinical guidelines on griefbot use — a striking absence given the technology's rapid commercialization.

The ethical concerns extend beyond individual psychology: consent of the deceased (most people leave no instructions about posthumous digital use), family disagreement (not all relatives want a digital recreation), commercial exploitation (subscription models create financial incentives to keep grieving people engaged), memory contamination (AI-edited content can implant false memories), and impacts on children who may lack the cognitive framework to distinguish simulation from reality.

## Part 2: Product landscape, technology, and design from first principles

### The grief tech market's most important signal is a pivot away from grief

The digital afterlife industry encompasses **57+ firms** with a broader digital legacy market valued at **$13–23 billion** and projected to reach $40–80 billion by 2030. But the most instructive data point is not a market size estimate — it's what happened to Eternos.

**Eternos**, founded in 2024 by Robert LoCascio (30-year veteran founder of LivePerson) with **$10.3 million in seed funding**, was positioned as the premium grief-tech platform: AI digital twins using voice cloning (300 phrases) and LLMs. LoCascio discovered that most people considering the product **were not preparing for death** — they wanted professional and productivity applications. He pivoted the entire company to **Uare.ai**, a personal AI platform for creators. This is the grief tech market's loudest signal: a well-funded, experienced founder with proven track record explicitly concluded that grief-only positioning cannot sustain a business.

The current product landscape breaks into several archetypes, each revealing different truths about what works:

**Pre-mortem voice/story capture** (HereAfter AI at $7.99/month) is the ethically cleanest model — the person records while alive, consent is clear. Users respond most emotionally to hearing their loved one's actual voice telling stories they'd never heard. The limitation: conversations feel scripted when the system hits knowledge boundaries. **Pre-recorded video Q&A** (StoryFile) offered authentic real-video answers matched to user queries — no generative AI, no confabulation risk. Its most compelling uses were institutional: Holocaust survivor testimonies, the woman who "attended" her own funeral. But StoryFile **filed for Chapter 11 bankruptcy**, owing approximately $4.5 million — another cautionary signal about business sustainability.

**AI chatbot simulations** (Project December, Replika, Seance AI) offer the most interactivity but the least authenticity. The Joshua Barbeau case — a man who created a GPT-powered chatbot of his deceased fiancée and eventually chose to stop, feeling he'd achieved closure — remains the most documented individual case. Importantly, Barbeau's use was self-limiting: healthy grief processing naturally reduced his need for the product. This creates a fundamental business paradox — **the better the product works at facilitating grief processing, the less the user needs it over time**.

### China is ahead on commercialization, and the reasons are structural

The Chinese market has moved faster than the West for three structural reasons: cultural alignment, cost structure, and existing infrastructure. **Silicon Intelligence (硅基智能)** offers basic digital avatars for as little as **$30 USD (199 yuan)** — roughly 100x cheaper than comparable Western products. Co-founder Sun Kai created an avatar of his late mother and talks to her regularly, deliberately disconnecting it from the internet so "she will always remain as the mother in my memory." **Super Brain (超级大脑)** has "resurrected" over 1,000 people, with orders spiking ahead of **Qingming Festival** (Tomb Sweeping Day) — a built-in cultural demand moment with no Western equivalent.

Chinese companies benefit from an existing AI avatar/streaming industry that has already solved many production challenges. Fu Shou Yuan, China's leading funeral operator, began offering digital memorials in 2023. The broader Chinese virtual digital human industry is projected to reach **270 billion yuan (~$37B) by 2030**. However, Chinese products also face ethical controversies: a Ningbo company created AI videos of deceased celebrities without consent, triggering intense backlash. China's deepfake regulations require notification and consent, though enforcement remains uncertain.

**DeepBrain AI** (South Korea) represents the premium tier: **$12,000–$24,000** to create a model, **$1,200 per video call** with the avatar. Their Re;memory2 product (2024) simplified dramatically to require only one photo and 10-second audio clip — but the avatar has essentially one "mood" and limited personality depth. The company's own admission of this limitation is revealing: visual fidelity is achievable, but personality depth remains the hard problem.

### What's working, what's failing, and the retention paradox

Five patterns consistently emerge from user feedback and market data. **What works**: authentic voice preservation (the single most valued feature across all platforms); closure for sudden/unexpected deaths (the strongest use case — saying goodbye, resolving unfinished business); story capture before death (families consistently regret not recording more); and bounded, self-limiting interactions rather than ongoing dependency. **What users find gimmicky**: spiritual/mystical framing (Seance AI), low-quality free tiers that damage the emotional experience, chatbots that hallucinate, and subscription models that feel exploitative.

The **retention paradox** is the central business challenge. Grief tech products consistently show a pattern of intense initial engagement followed by declining use. Cambridge researchers found that even users who initially find comfort may get "drained by daily interactions that become an overwhelming emotional weight." The Korean VR mother who was reunited with her deceased daughter via VR reported the experience was helpful but "has no interest in repeating it — she prefers handwritten letters." This pattern suggests that grief tech may be psychologically *working as intended* — facilitating processing that naturally reduces the need for the product — but this creates an inherently self-liquidating customer relationship that challenges subscription models.

The most sustainable business models appear to be institutional/B2B licensing (StoryFile's museum and education partnerships before bankruptcy) and ultra-low-cost one-time-fee mass market (Chinese companies at $30). Pure B2C subscription for grief tech faces the fundamental problem that healthy grieving is self-limiting.

### The technology stack is ready — with one critical bottleneck

The technical feasibility of a holographic memorial device has crossed a threshold in 2025–2026, though with important constraints.

**Voice cloning** is mature. A 2025 Cambridge study found AI systems replicate **up to 95% of subtle vocal characteristics**. ElevenLabs' Professional Voice Clone captures cadence, tonality, pauses, and breaths with near-indistinguishable quality from 30+ minutes of clean audio. Latency has dropped to **75ms** with Flash models — fast enough for conversational interaction. The critical limitation: quality depends entirely on input data. Phone recordings and noisy audio produce robotic results. For families of the deceased, available audio (voicemails, home videos, phone recordings) is often low quality.

**Persona creation** works best with a hybrid approach: fine-tuning an LLM for personality and speech patterns (which "gives it the persona") combined with RAG for specific memories and knowledge (which "gives it the knowledge"). CarynAI demonstrated that 2,000 hours of content produces a convincing persona — but most bereaved families have access to text messages, social media posts, and some photos/videos, far short of ideal. This data gap is the primary bottleneck, not the AI itself.

**Image-to-video** is commercially available through HeyGen, Kling AI, and Vozo AI. A single photo can be animated into a talking video with lip-sync. Quality is good for short clips but degrades over extended duration, and emotional expression range remains limited.

**Holographic display** has reached a consumer-viable inflection point. The **Looking Glass Musubi** (announced March 2026, shipping June 2026) converts any 2D photo or video to a holographic display in a 7-inch frame at **$99–$149** — a gift-viable price point. It uses Gaussian splatting AI for local processing, stores 1,000 holograms, and offers 170° viewing angle with no glasses required. However, this is a light-field display within a frame, not a free-standing Star Wars hologram — managing expectations is critical. True volumetric holography remains research-only at consumer prices.

**The primary technical bottleneck is real-time interactive video generation.** Current video generation takes seconds to minutes per clip. A real-time conversational holographic avatar that responds naturally is not feasible in 2026. The achievable path is animated photo + voice interaction — the holographic frame shows a living 3D image of the person while the AI voice responds conversationally.

### A viable product design, built from psychological first principles

The research points to two "most rigid" needs in memorialization that technology could authentically serve: **(1) Authentic voice preservation and story capture** — hearing the real voice is the single most emotionally powerful feature across all existing products, and aligns with continuing bonds theory's emphasis on tangible connection. **(2) Closure for sudden or unexpected deaths** — the ability to say things left unsaid, ask for forgiveness, or express love maps directly onto Bowlby's "yearning and searching" phase and is the use case with the strongest empirical support from the Xygkou et al. study.

**A well-designed product would be a "holographic memory frame" — a dedicated device** combining a Looking Glass-class holographic display (showing 3D photos/videos), an ElevenLabs-powered voice clone, and a RAG-based conversational AI trained on the family's uploaded memories. Hardware cost: **$199–$349**. It would operate in three modes: a passive "memory mode" cycling holographic photos (the always-on default), a "story mode" where the AI shares pre-recorded or reconstructed stories in the person's voice, and a "conversation mode" activated on demand.

The authenticity-interactivity balance should use what I'd call **"guided rails with transparency."** The system should clearly distinguish between things the person actually said or recorded (flagged as "direct memory") and AI-generated responses ("inspired by" their personality and knowledge). This directly addresses the false memory contamination risk identified by Julia Shaw while preserving interactivity. The AI should never confabulate facts about the person's life — when it doesn't know something, it should say so honestly, perhaps with: "I don't have a memory of that, but here's something I do remember about..."

**Avoiding the novelty-to-shelf-decoration problem requires four design principles**: dual-purpose utility (even without AI, it's a beautiful 3D photo display of a loved one — a worthy memorial object on its own), calendar-awareness (proactively surface memories on birthdays, anniversaries, and cultural observance days like Qingming or yahrzeit), family ecosystem (multiple family members contribute memories and access the device), and no subscription lock-in for core features (photos and voice should work offline; AI conversation as optional premium). The device should integrate into existing cultural memorial practices rather than replacing them.

### Where the psychology constrains the product design

Three psychological principles from the research should serve as hard design constraints.

First, **the Dual Process Model demands oscillation.** A product that encourages unlimited, always-available interaction with the deceased risks trapping users in loss-orientation. Design implication: the product should have natural boundaries. A daily interaction limit, a "take a break" nudge after extended sessions, or a gradual transition protocol where the AI gently encourages restoration-oriented activity would respect the DPM's insight that both engagement and respite are necessary.

Second, **the internalized-versus-externalized bonds framework demands a transition arc.** The product should not become a permanent fixture of daily externalized interaction. It should be designed to facilitate what Field's research identifies as healthy: the gradual internalization of the bond. Over weeks and months, the AI might shift from active conversation toward sharing memories, asking the user questions about their own life, and eventually suggesting the user write their own thoughts rather than seeking the AI's response. A well-designed grief tech product would, counterintuitively, **design for its own diminishing use over time**.

Third, **the 10–20% PGD prevalence rate means some users will be clinically vulnerable.** The product should include monitoring for warning signs (daily engagement increasing rather than decreasing after 3+ months, expressions of inability to function, statements suggesting confusion between the AI and the real person) and clear pathways to professional support. Lindemann's and Klugman's proposals that griefbots could be classified as medical devices may be premature, but building in clinical safety features is not.

## Conclusion: where opportunity meets constraint

The grief tech market sits at a genuine inflection point where technological capability has outpaced psychological understanding. Voice cloning, conversational AI, and holographic displays are all consumer-ready, but the academic evidence on whether digital recreations help or hinder grief remains almost nonexistent — one rigorous study with 10 participants is the field's best evidence. This means any company entering this space is participating in what Cambridge researchers call "a vast techno-cultural experiment."

The most defensible product strategy combines three insights: **(1)** Pre-mortem capture is ethically and psychologically superior to posthumous reconstruction, but most demand comes after death — so the product must gracefully handle both. **(2)** The business must not depend on grief alone — the Eternos pivot confirms that grief-only positioning is commercially fragile. A dual-purpose device (beautiful memorial object + AI interaction) or a broader personal legacy platform that includes grief as one use case is structurally sounder. **(3)** The Chinese market's combination of lower costs, cultural infrastructure (Qingming), and existing digital human technology makes it the natural first-mover market, while Western markets need more ethical scaffolding before mainstream adoption.

The deepest insight from the psychological literature is also the most commercially uncomfortable: **the healthiest grief is self-limiting.** A product that genuinely serves the bereaved's psychological needs will see engagement naturally decline over time. This is not a bug — it is the clearest possible signal that the product is working. The companies that build for this reality, rather than optimizing against it, will earn the trust that grief technology demands.
